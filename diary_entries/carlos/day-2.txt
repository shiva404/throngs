09:00 AM - 11:45 AM  [165 min] TAX PLANNING SESSION — NAKAMURA RESTAURANT GROUP
  Video call with Ken Nakamura. Ken runs two restaurants: Eastside Ramen (7 years old,
  mature and profitable) and Harbor Fusion (18 months old, growing but still burning
  cash). He's a smart operator but not a numbers person — I have to translate everything
  into plain language.

  WHAT WE COVERED:

  1. Year-over-year P&L comparison.
     2022 net income: $148,000 (combined)
     2023 net income: $171,000 (combined, Harbor Fusion opened mid-year)
     2024 net income: $127,000 (combined — Harbor Fusion's startup costs drag the total)

  Ken was alarmed by the 2024 drop. I walked him through it: Harbor Fusion had $94,000
  in startup and ramp-up costs in 2024 (lease improvements, equipment, initial staffing
  overhang). On a standalone basis, Eastside Ramen posted $162,000 in net income in 2024,
  up from $157,000 in 2023 — strong. Harbor Fusion lost $35,000 in 2024, but that's
  actually ahead of Ken's original projections by about $18,000. He hadn't looked at
  it that way. That's why he needs an accountant, not just a bookkeeper.

  2. Cash flow — where does the money go?
  This was Ken's main question from yesterday's call. The answer is: accounts payable
  timing, food inventory build-up, and owner distributions that don't match net income.

  In Q3 2024, Ken drew $48,000 from the business. Net income that quarter: $31,200.
  He drew $16,800 more than the business earned that quarter. His cash balance dropped
  $22,000 in Q3. He didn't understand why. Now he does.

  We built a simple cash flow projection for 2025 together. Recommendation: limit owner
  draws to no more than 80% of trailing 3-month average net income. I'll build him a
  simple rolling calculator in Excel.

  3. 2024 tax liability estimate.
  Ken is a sole proprietor (Schedule C, not S-Corp). I've been suggesting he elect
  S-Corp status for two years. At his current net income level (~$162,000 from Eastside
  alone), the self-employment tax savings from an S-Corp structure would be meaningful —
  roughly $8,000–$12,000 per year, depending on how we set reasonable compensation.
  He always says "let me think about it." Today I put the numbers in front of him on
  screen: SE tax on $162,000 as a sole prop = $22,876. SE tax equivalent if structured
  as S-Corp with $85,000 salary = $12,003. Savings: $10,873 per year. Plus the payroll
  tax costs of running an S-Corp (maybe $1,200/year). Net annual savings: approximately
  $9,600.

  He said he wants to do it. Told him I'll send an S-Corp election checklist and the
  Form 2553 instructions. This is a meaningful win — I've been patient on this one.

  4. Quarterly estimates for Q1 2025.
  We confirmed the $9,400 federal / $2,350 state amounts I calculated yesterday. Ken
  will send the checks today.
