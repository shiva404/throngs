08:00 AM - 08:40 AM  [40 min] MORNING REVIEW — INSTINCT CONFIRMED

  Opened QB Accountant dashboard at 8:00. Coffee in hand. My usual morning scan: pulled
  up the Transactions page for Ortega Medical Supply (quarterly client, Q1 review due
  this week) to get a read on the bank feed before scheduling the full review.

  First thing I noticed: a $1,450 charge from a medical equipment supplier —
  "Cardinal Health" — was categorized by QB as "Meals & Entertainment."

  Cardinal Health is a major medical supply distributor. They have never, in 14 years of
  practice, been a restaurant. QB's ML engine apparently saw an unfamiliar vendor name
  in a transaction and made an inference based on... something. The transaction description
  was "CARDINAL HLTH INC ECOM 614-757-5000" — not exactly ambiguous. The phone number
  is Cardinal Health's corporate line.

  This is the kind of thing that keeps me up at night: if I weren't reviewing these
  transactions carefully, a client's P&L would show a $1,450 meal. Their margins would
  look wrong. Their COGS would be understated. Year-end, their tax return would be wrong.

  I suspected this was not isolated. I had a feeling something changed in QB's
  auto-categorization model recently — I've been seeing more misfires than usual.
  Today, I'm going to find out how widespread this is.

08:40 AM - 11:15 AM  [155 min] AUDIT: QB AUTO-CATEGORIZATION FAILURES ACROSS 5 CLIENTS

  ⬛ QUICKBOOKS ONLINE SESSIONS 1–5 (CLIENTS: Ortega Medical, Nakamura, Henderson,
     Bloom Legal, Chen's Bakery — audit pass)  [08:40 AM – 11:15 AM, 155 min]

  I spent the next 2 hours and 35 minutes running a methodical audit of auto-categorized
  transactions for five clients, focusing on January and February 2025 (the period after
  what I suspect was a QB model update). Here is what I found:

  --- CLIENT: Ortega Medical Supply ---
  Reviewed 84 auto-categorized transactions (January–February).
  Errors found: 11

  1. Cardinal Health × 4 transactions: $1,450, $2,340, $890, $3,100 — all auto-categorized
     as "Meals & Entertainment." Should be: Medical Supplies (COGS). Total misfire: $7,780.
  2. McKesson Corporation × 3 transactions: $4,200, $1,670, $2,950 — categorized as
     "Office Supplies." McKesson is also a medical distributor. Should be: Medical Supplies.
  3. FEDEX GROUND ECOM payment: $312 — categorized as "Advertising." FedEx is a shipping
     cost. Should be: Shipping & Delivery.
  4. A $750 payment to "Compliance Training Associates" — categorized as "Meals &
     Entertainment." This is CE (continuing education) for Ortega's licensed staff.
     Should be: Professional Development.
  5. Recurring monthly payment to a practice management software: $299/month × 2 =
     $598 — categorized as "Professional Fees." Should be: Software Subscriptions.

  Aggregate financial impact if uncorrected:
  - COGS understated by ~$16,930
  - Meals & Entertainment overstated by ~$9,880 (a red flag in any audit)
  - Gross profit overstated by ~$16,930

  This is not a rounding error. This is a materially wrong P&L.

  I corrected all 11 errors. For Cardinal Health and McKesson, I also updated the
  vendor records with the correct default category so QB should learn the correct
  mapping going forward. I have done this before. I am not confident it will hold.

  --- CLIENT: Nakamura Restaurant Group ---
  Reviewed 127 transactions (January–February, combined both locations).
  Errors found: 8

  For a restaurant, the irony: QB correctly categorized most of Nakamura's actual food
  vendors. But:
  1. US Foods (a restaurant food distributor) × 3 transactions: $3,400, $2,200, $4,100 —
     all correctly categorized as "Food & Beverage (COGS)." These were fine.
  2. DoorDash and UberEats commission fees × 4 transactions: QB categorized these as
     "Sales" (revenue). They are NOT revenue. They are contra-revenue commissions —
     merchant fees paid to the delivery platform. Should be booked as "Merchant Fees"
     (expense). This is a $1,847 error in the wrong direction — it overstates revenue
     and understates expenses simultaneously, a double impact on net income.
  3. One transaction: $240 to "National Restaurant Association" — categorized as
     "Dues & Subscriptions." Correct! But the wrong sub-account — I have Nakamura
     set up with "Industry Associations" as a sub-account. Minor but I fixed it.

  The DoorDash/UberEats issue is particularly concerning because it repeats monthly.
  I had set up the correct category for these vendors. If QB's ML model is overriding
  my manual rules, that is a serious problem. I went into the vendor settings and
  confirmed that my manual rules were still saved. QB is not overriding them for new
  transactions — it apparently did a one-time re-categorization of historical transactions
  I had already confirmed. I have never seen this behavior before. I will document it.

01:00 PM - 03:20 PM  [140 min] WESTBROOK FITNESS — DEEP DIVE LOCATION 2 (MILLBROOK)

  ⬛ QUICKBOOKS ONLINE SESSIONS 6–7 (CLIENT: Westbrook Fitness)  [01:05 PM – 03:15 PM, 130 min]

  Sarah's Location 2 — Millbrook — has thin margins. P&L shows it's profitable but only
  barely. Gross margin: 58% vs. 67% at Location 1 and 71% at Location 3. I wanted to
  understand why.

  Pulled general ledger detail for Millbrook for all of 2024. This is where QB's
  transaction-level drill-down earns its keep — I can see every single entry, in
  chronological order, with memo fields, amounts, and categories. When the data is
  clean, this view is excellent.

  What I found after 90 minutes of detailed review:

  1. Instructor compensation at Millbrook is higher as a % of revenue than the other
     two locations — 29% vs. 22% at Location 1, 19% at Location 3. This is likely a
     combination of class size (Millbrook's classes are smaller — it's a smaller space)
     and the instructor mix (Millbrook has more senior, higher-paid instructors).
     This is a management decision, not an accounting error, but it explains the margin.

  2. Rent at Millbrook: $4,800/month vs. $3,200 at Location 1, $3,600 at Location 3.
     Millbrook is in a higher-rent district. This is baked in until the lease renews
     (September 2026). Sarah will need to decide whether to renegotiate aggressively
     or consider whether Millbrook's revenue can support expansion within the space
     (more classes per day). Revenue opportunity, not an accounting issue.

  3. One genuine accounting issue: cleaning service at Millbrook ($480/month) has been
     categorized inconsistently — sometimes "Janitorial Services," sometimes "Outside
     Services," sometimes "General & Administrative." I standardized to "Facilities &
     Maintenance" across all 12 months and across all three locations for comparability.
     This affects expense categorization but not total expenses or net income.

  4. Equipment maintenance at Millbrook: $8,400 in 2024, vs. $3,100 at Location 1 and
     $1,800 at Location 3. This is high. Pulled the transaction detail — 14 separate
     service calls to "ProFit Equipment Repair." I cross-referenced the QB asset register:
     Millbrook's treadmills and ellipticals are 6–7 years old. They're failing. Sarah
     needs to make a capital expenditure decision — repair costs are approaching the
     threshold where replacement makes more financial sense.

  Prepared a one-page memo for Sarah with this analysis. Key finding: Millbrook's margin
  compression is primarily structural (rent, instructor mix, aging equipment), not an
  operational inefficiency. Recommendation: model the economics of replacing the
  cardio equipment (Section 179 depreciation possible) and negotiate the lease. Will
  send tomorrow.

END OF DAY — CARLOS'S REFLECTION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  The most frustrating kind of problem is the invisible one. If a client's P&L is
  obviously wrong — revenue is negative, expenses are 500% of the prior year — someone
  will notice. But $7,780 of medical supply purchases showing up as "Meals & Entertainment"
  across four transactions over two months? That's the kind of error that passes a casual
  review. It only shows up if you drill down into the transaction-level detail and ask:
  does this category make sense for this vendor?

  I audit every confirmed transaction for monthly clients. For quarterly clients, I do a
  spot audit. For annual clients... I review what I review. If QB's ML model is
  retroactively re-categorizing confirmed transactions on annual-review clients, I may
  not catch it until December. That is a problem.

  I am going to add a step to my monthly and quarterly close checklist: run a
  "transactions changed after confirmation" audit. If QB won't tell me which confirmed
  transactions were retroactively re-categorized (it doesn't; there's no audit log for
  this), I'll need to either trust my prior exports and diff against current state, or
  add it to the standard review scope. More work created by a "smart" feature.

  The positive: QB's general ledger drill-down saved me hours of manual work on
  Westbrook's Millbrook analysis. I could not have done that analysis as efficiently
  in any other way at this price point. QB has real strengths. Today was not one of them.
