  ⬛ QUICKBOOKS ONLINE SESSION 2 (CLIENT: Pioneer Auto Parts)  [08:35 AM – 09:10 AM, 35 min]

  Then I found something new. While digging into the matching rules, I noticed that QB's
  bank matching algorithm was applying a rule for a different client — a rule I set up
  for a vendor at Bloom Legal Services — to Pioneer's transactions. Not in terms of
  categorization (the categories were right) but in terms of suggested match confidence.
  QB was showing "High Confidence" matches on Pioneer transactions that weren't confident
  at all — and when I looked at the rule IDs in the QB API (I accessed this via the QB

  the wrong client's rule set.

  This is a cross-client data contamination in the matching algorithm's confidence scoring.
  QB Accountant manages multiple client files, and the matching engine appears to be, in
  some cases, applying one client's learned rules to a different client's bank feed — not
  the categorizations themselves (which are isolated per client), but the confidence
  metadata. This would explain why I've been seeing unusually high "confidence" matches
  on transactions that shouldn't be confident, across multiple clients this week.

  This is a real bug. This is a significant bug. I need to document it carefully and
  report it properly.

  Replied to Dave: "Dave — I've identified the root cause of the Michigan Auto Distributors

09:10 AM - 10:05 AM  [55 min] QB BUG INVESTIGATION — BANK MATCHING ALGORITHM

  ⬛ QUICKBOOKS ONLINE SESSION 3 (DIAGNOSTIC)  [09:15 AM – 10:00 AM, 45 min]

  Before posting to QB Community, I wanted to be sure of what I was seeing. I tested
  across three client files — Pioneer Auto Parts, Ortega Medical Supply, and Nakamura
  Restaurant Group — by uploading identical test transactions and observing the matching
  behavior and confidence scores.

  Findings:
  1. For a test transaction with description "CARDINAL HEALTH INC" in Ortega's account:
     QB showed "High Confidence: Medical Supplies." Correct — I trained this rule
     Wednesday. Expected behavior.

  2. For the same test transaction in Nakamura's account (where Cardinal Health has
     no history and no rule): QB showed "High Confidence: Medical Supplies."
     INCORRECT. Nakamura should not have any knowledge of Cardinal Health. High
     confidence on an untrained vendor in a restaurant's account is wrong.

  3. For a test transaction "GUSTO NET PAY 1234" in Nakamura's account: QB showed
     "Medium Confidence: Payroll Expenses." Correct — Nakamura uses Gusto.

  4. For the same test transaction in Pioneer's account (Pioneer uses ADP, not Gusto):
     QB showed "Medium Confidence: Payroll Expenses." The confidence is right but the
     source should be Pioneer's own history, not a bleed from Nakamura's Gusto rules.

  The pattern is consistent: QB Accountant's matching confidence engine is sharing rule
  metadata across client accounts. The actual category assignments are client-isolated
  (I tested this — it's not categorizing Pioneer as a restaurant), but the confidence
  scores and rule-match signals are bleeding across the multi-client accountant view.

  This means:
  - Transactions in new client accounts may be shown as "High Confidence" even when QB
    has no basis for that confidence in that specific client's history.
  - Practitioners who rely on confidence scores to batch-approve bank feed transactions
    may approve incorrect matches because QB falsely signals confidence.
  - The Pioneer bank feed alias bug may be a symptom of this same issue: my alias rule
    is saving correctly but firing incorrectly against the wrong matching logic.
