09:35 AM - 10:15 AM  [40 min] QB ONLINE — BANKING RULES — SUNSET HARDWARE

  ⬛ QUICKBOOKS ONLINE SESSION 2  [09:35 AM – 10:15 AM, 40 min]

  Decided to set up banking rules for Sunset Hardware while I was thinking
  about it. Banking rules tell QBO: "When a transaction matches these criteria,
  automatically assign this category." In Desktop, I handled all categorization
  manually and I was fine with that. But if Dennis is going to keep entering
  things himself via the mobile app and the bank feed is pulling transactions
  daily, rules can prevent miscategorizations from piling up.

  Found the Rules feature: Transactions > Rules > New Rule.

  Created five rules:

  Rule 1: ACE DISTRIBUTOR (when description contains "ACE DIST")
    → Default account: 50000 Cost of Goods Sold
    → Payee: Ace Distributor
    → Condition: Amount > $100 (to exclude small returns/credits)

  Rule 2: HOME DEPOT ("HOME DEPOT" in description)
    → Default account: 60800 Repairs & Maintenance (they use this for
      store fixture supplies; not to be confused with his inventory)
    → Actually — this one is tricky. Sometimes it's supplies, sometimes
      repairs, sometimes COGS-adjacent. I decided NOT to create this rule
      because it's too inconsistent. Better I review these manually.

  Rule 3: BP GAS ("BP #" in description)
    → Default account: Auto Expense
    → Payee: BP Gas

  Rule 4: WELLS FARGO FEE ("WF MONTHLY SERVICE" in description)
    → Default account: Bank Fees & Charges
    → Payee: Wells Fargo

  Rule 5: AT&T ("ATT BUSINESS" in description)
    → Default account: Telephone
    → Payee: AT&T Business

  The Rules interface in QBO is cleaner than I expected — better than the
  clunky "Memorized Transactions" feature in Desktop, which honestly was
  never very intuitive. I'll give QBO credit here: the rules setup is logical.
  "If/Then" style. I understood it without reading the help article.

  This took 40 minutes including testing (I re-processed one of the auto-pulled
  April transactions through the rule to make sure it assigned correctly).

END OF DAY — MARTHA'S REFLECTION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Here is what I know after this week: I can do this. QBO is not my friend
  yet, but it is becoming less of a stranger.

  Let me count what went right:
    - Three reconciliations completed, all to zero difference
    - Q1 summary for Parker Dental delivered to the CPA on time
    - FUTA problem caught and documented before it became a bigger issue
    - Riverview Café onboarding underway — October and November complete
    - Banking rules set up for Sunset Hardware (should save me time next month)
    - Bella's bank feed is finally reconnected
    - Dennis's expense guide written and ready to mail
    - All reports printed, all binders filed

  What still bothers me about QBO:

  The menu changes with no warning. On Tuesday I felt like I'd come into the
  office and someone had moved my filing cabinet. "Banking" is now
  "Transactions." Fine. But tell me first.

  The permission structure for payroll. I know why it exists. I still don't
  like it. A bookkeeper with fifteen years of experience should have more
  access, not less.

  The bank feed authentication. Every time a connection breaks, fixing it
  requires the client — not me. In a busy small business, the owner doesn't
  always have time to log into a third-party app and go through
  multi-factor authentication steps. This creates a gap in the workflow.

  What I'll say is genuinely better in QBO than Desktop:
    - Access anywhere. I can check a client's account from home if needed.
      I haven't done this yet, but I could. In Desktop, the company file
      was on one machine.
    - The bank feed itself, when working, is faster than importing .QBO files.
    - Banking rules are more elegant than Desktop's memorized transactions.
    - The audit trail on every transaction (who changed what and when) is
      actually excellent. I caught Dennis's price adjustment because of it.
    - The custom report groups are going to save me time once I build them all.

  If I'm being honest: QBO is a capable piece of software. My frustrations
  this week were mostly about the unfamiliarity, the few genuine design gaps,
  and one QBO-caused payroll error. That's not a bad week. I've had worse in
  Desktop.

  I'm fifty-eight years old and I've been doing this for thirty-one years.
  I've survived the move from paper ledgers to DOS-based software, from DOS
  to Windows, from floppy disks to network drives, from paper bank statements
  to electronic ones. I survived QuickBooks Desktop's thirty-seven updates
  over fifteen years. I will survive QuickBooks Online.

  The numbers balance. That's what matters.
