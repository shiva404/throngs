  But the FUTA rate field was empty.

  Payroll Support or have your payroll admin approve this change."

  Payroll admin. That would be Dr. Parker herself, since it's her company file.
  I don't have admin rights on the payroll module — I have accountant access,
  which is different. In Desktop, with the old payroll add-on, I had full
  access. Here there's an "accountant" permission level that doesn't allow
  payroll tax rate changes. This is a known limitation that I accept, but
  it means I need to get Dr. Parker on the phone.

  Six completed runs. I clicked into each and looked at the tax detail line
  by line. FUTA line: on all six runs it showed $0.00. The rate field was
  indeed blank for all of Q1.

  Deep breath. This is a real problem. FUTA should have been withheld and
  remitted. FUTA is an employer-only tax (not withheld from employee wages),
  so the employees aren't affected. But Dr. Parker owes FUTA for the year.
  The total Q1 gross wages were approximately $85,773.70. FUTA at 6.0% on
  first $7,000 per employee per year...

  Let me calculate manually (I always double-check payroll math on paper):
  7 employees × $7,000 = $49,000 total FUTA-taxable wages maximum per year.
  For Q1, all employees are still under $7,000 for the year if we spread
  $85,773.70 across 7 employees — average $12,248 per employee for the quarter.
  But FUTA only applies to first $7,000 per employee per YEAR.

  Checking individual wages from the payroll detail:
    Dr. Parker:         $22,500.00 Q1  → FUTA taxable: $7,000
    Dr. Nguyen:         $18,750.00 Q1  → FUTA taxable: $7,000
    Office Mgr:          $9,750.00 Q1  → FUTA taxable: $7,000
    Hygienist 1:         $9,750.00 Q1  → FUTA taxable: $7,000
    Hygienist 2:         $9,750.00 Q1  → FUTA taxable: $7,000
    Dental Asst. 1:      $7,500.00 Q1  → FUTA taxable: $7,000
    Dental Asst. 2:      $8,773.70 Q1  → FUTA taxable: $7,000
  Total FUTA taxable wages Q1: $49,000.00
  FUTA owed: $49,000 × 6.0% = $2,940.00
  Less standard 5.4% FUTA credit (for SUTA paid): $49,000 × 5.4% = $2,646.00
  Net FUTA liability: $294.00

  That's actually a manageable number — the net effective FUTA rate after the
  credit is just 0.6%. But it still needs to be paid and the 941/940 forms
  will need to reflect this. This is beyond my role — I handle books, not
  payroll tax filings. But I need to flag it to both Dr. Parker and her CPA.

  At this point I needed to call QBO Payroll Support to understand why the
  FUTA field got cleared and whether the past payroll runs can be retroactively
  corrected in QBO's payroll records (or if corrections need to happen at the
  federal level via 941-X amendment).

END OF DAY — MARTHA'S REFLECTION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Long day. Harder day than yesterday, mostly because of the payroll situation.
  I know bookkeeping and I know accounting. I knew exactly what the problem was
  the moment I saw the blank FUTA field — I just needed software access to fix
  it, and the software said no.

  That's the thing about QBO that nobody tells you when they're selling you the
  upgrade from Desktop: you lose some control. In Desktop, I was the one in
  charge. I had administrator access to everything. Here, there are "roles" and
  "permissions" and certain things only the business owner can approve, even if
  the bookkeeper is the one who knows what's actually wrong.

  I don't doubt that the permission structure is there for a good reason —
  probably to prevent fraud. But in a small business context where the
  bookkeeper is trusted and the owner is busy filling cavities all day, it
  slows everything down.

  Dr. Parker is a good client and she handled it graciously. The books are
  clean. The Q1 summary is done. The CPA has everything he needs. I'll call
  it a win, even if it took 41 minutes of hold music to get there.
