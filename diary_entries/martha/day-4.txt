  First task: enter opening balances. In QBO, the cleanest way to set opening
  balances is through a journal entry on the first day of operations. I've
  read three different QBO help articles on this over the past two weeks and
  they all say slightly different things. In Desktop, opening balances were set
  in the "Set Up Chart of Accounts" wizard — simple and guided. In QBO there's
  no wizard. You either (a) use the Opening Balance field on each account when
  you create it, or (b) enter a journal entry.

  Since I already created the accounts without opening balances (I forgot to
  enter them at the time — this was my own mistake), I needed to do option (b):
  a journal entry.

  New > Journal Entry. Date: 10/01/2023.

  Journal entry for opening balances:
    DEBIT   10100 Checking          $8,200.00
    DEBIT   10200 Savings           $2,500.00
    DEBIT   15000 Equipment         $52,400.00
    CREDIT  21000 Loans Payable     $45,000.00
    CREDIT  30000 Owner's Equity    $18,100.00
    Total debits: $63,100.00. Total credits: $63,100.00. ✓

  Saved the journal entry. QBO accepted it without complaint.

  Then I tried to set the opening balance for the bank reconciliation. Each
  account's first reconciliation starting point needs to reflect the 10/01
  balance. In QBO, when you first reconcile an account, it asks for the opening
  balance. Good. I'll handle that when I get to the October reconciliation.

  Next: I needed to add the two Suncoast bank accounts as bank feeds. But — and
  I learned from Monday's Bella's Florist experience — Riverview Café does not
  have QBO bank feed set up. I'm not going to try today. I'll enter everything

  I entered transactions fairly quickly, but QBO's expense entry form has a
  persistent annoyance: when I tab through the fields and reach the "Account"
  dropdown, I have to type the account name or number. It doesn't remember the
  last account I used for the same vendor the way Desktop used to. Desktop
  would auto-populate "last used account" for any vendor you'd entered before.
  In QBO, if the vendor has a default account set up in the vendor profile,
  it remembers. But for new vendors with no history (like all of these), I'm
  typing the account name every single time.

  I spent fifteen minutes halfway through November setting up default expense
  accounts for each vendor in their QBO Vendor records. Expenses > Vendors >
  click each vendor > Edit > "Default expense account." Once set, QBO does
  auto-fill. Should have done this at the start of the day. Live and learn.

END OF DAY — MARTHA'S REFLECTION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Tedious. There's no other word for six months of manual retroactive data
  entry. But it's honest work and it has to be done right.

  I keep thinking about how this would go in Desktop. Same amount of entry,
  honestly. But Desktop felt more... controllable. Like I was building something
  in a space I understood. QBO feels like I'm working inside someone else's
  house and I have to ask permission to move the furniture.

  The thing I appreciated today: QBO's ability to add new accounts mid-session
  without interrupting my flow. I created two new accounts (Customer Deposits
  and Marketing & Advertising) on the fly without closing what I was working
  on. In older versions of Desktop you sometimes had to close a transaction
  to go update the chart of accounts and then reopen. Small thing, but genuine
  improvement.

  The vendor default account setup — I should have done that first thing
  this morning. Next time I onboard a new client in QBO, that's step two
  right after the COA is set up. Lesson learned.

  The Benedettis are going to be pleased when I can show them November's net
  income of $6,788.05. That's their first profitable month. Six months in,
