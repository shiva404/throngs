08:05 AM - 09:00 AM  [55 min] QUICKBOOKS ONLINE — SESSION 1

  ⬛ QUICKBOOKS ONLINE SESSION 1  [08:05 AM – 08:52 AM, 47 min]

  Opened the Expenses section. Sorted by amount, descending. Looking for
  anything suspicious.

  There it is. Line item: $8,100.00 — "Meta Ads / Social Advertising"
  — February 14th.

  We don't run Meta ads. We have never run Meta ads. I'm a B2B SaaS
  company. My customers are not browsing Instagram.

  This is the AWS bill. I'm sure of it. Same dollar amount. Same date.
  It's been sitting under "Marketing — Advertising" instead of
  "Infrastructure / Software & Subscriptions" for six weeks.

  Why does this matter? Because my Q1 P&L shows $0 in infrastructure
  for February, and an unexplained $8,100 spike in marketing. And if
  the board looks at the expense breakdown — which Harrington definitely
  will — that is going to look extremely weird. "You spent $8,100 on
  Meta ads in February and then $0 the next month?"

  How did this happen? I have a theory: when the AWS charge came in,
  whoever (Carol? The QB auto-categorization AI?) matched it to
  "Meta" because AWS's billing descriptor on the bank statement says
  "AMZN" and the auto-categorize function was... drunk?

  Actually, I think I know exactly what happened. Last fall I was
  experimenting with a tiny $50 Facebook campaign for awareness. It's
  the only "advertising" charge we've ever had. QB's auto-categorize
  learned that pattern and then six months later saw a large charge
  and went, "advertising! I know advertising!"

  I have built a product that prevents exactly this kind of data
  misclassification for our customers. The irony is fully lost on nobody.

  Tried to fix it myself. Clicked on the transaction. Saw the category
  dropdown. Changed it from "Marketing — Advertising" to... wait, there
  are 47 sub-categories in here. Where does AWS go? "Software &
  Subscriptions"? "IT & Software"? "Computer & Internet"? These are all
  different options and I genuinely do not know which is correct.

  Googled: "quickbooks online how to categorize aws charges"
  Found a forum thread. People are debating whether cloud infrastructure
  is "Cost of Goods Sold" or an operating expense. There's a long
  argument in the comments. Someone said "it depends on whether it's
  directly attributable to revenue generation." I closed the tab.

  Called Carol.

  Carol (phone, 8:42 AM): "Jake what happened."

  Me: "There's an $8,100 charge under Marketing that I'm pretty sure
  is the February AWS bill. I want to fix it but I don't know which
  category it goes in."

  Carol: (pause) "Do NOT touch it. I'll fix it remotely this morning.
  And Jake — did you change anything else in that transaction?"

  Me: "I opened the dropdown but I didn't save anything."

  Carol: "You're sure?"

  Me: "...I'm like 95% sure."

  Carol: "I'll check the audit log."

  (There's an audit log. Of course there's an audit log. Carol has been
  watching everything I've ever done in QB and I'm only learning this now.)

  Carol said she'd have it fixed within the hour and she'd also check
  if there were any other auto-categorization errors from Q1 while she
  was in there. I said thank you and also I'm sorry and she said "it's
  fine, this is literally my job."

  Carol is worth every dollar I pay her and also I should pay her more.

  While waiting for Carol to fix things, I kept myself busy by looking
  at the QB dashboard cash flow widget. Cash in vs cash out for the
  trailing 30 days looked healthy. I am capable of reading this widget
  correctly and it brings me peace.

  Then I accidentally hovered over a line in the P&L report and a tooltip
  appeared that said "Adjust this using a journal entry." I did not
  click it. I have learned.

  ⬛ QUICKBOOKS ONLINE SESSION 2  [10:20 AM – 10:38 AM, 18 min]

  Checked in to see if Carol had fixed the AWS miscategorization.
  She had. The $8,100 is now sitting under "Cost of Revenue — Cloud

  Q1 expenses revised:

  Wait, that's a bigger loss than I thought. Revenue $57,300, expenses
  $186,300... that's a net loss of $129,000?

  No wait. That can't be right. Payroll alone is $135K but we're spending
  $1.8M in Series A money, that's different from the P&L I think?

  Texted Carol: "Hey the expense total is now $186K which gives us a
  loss of $129K for Q1 — is that right? That seems like a lot."

  Carol (three minutes later): "Yes that's roughly right. Series A funds
  don't appear on the P&L — they're on the balance sheet as equity.

  $43K/month in operating loss. That's your burn rate."

  Me: "So the burn rate is $43K, not $54K like I said in the deck?"

  Carol: "Depends how you define it. $43K is net burn (loss). $57K is
  gross cash out. Both are valid to report. Just be clear which one
  you're using and be consistent."

  for approximately four minutes.
